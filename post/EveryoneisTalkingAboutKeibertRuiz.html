Everyone is Talking About Keibert Ruiz

June 10, 2026

Two weeks ago, Keibert Ruiz was the talk of the baseball internet with another great article from Spencer Nusbaum eliciting a detailed write-up on FanGraphs. If you had read my post a month ago you would know that after being burned the last two years, I was highly skeptical of the Keiberissance. However, faced with insurmountable evidence that something might be up, and waiting through some amateur blogger delays, I took a look at the data again and found something both interesting and missed by the pros.

Both Nusbaum and the FanGraphs post focused a lot on the change in Ruiz’s stats batting from the right side and left side, which is natural. That’s a pretty common split to find on most statistical leaderboards and there is a real difference, both this year and throughout his career. However, I think focusing on Ruiz’s platoon splits is actually missing what the Nats front office and coaches are having him change, how it’s making an impact, and what it means going forward for both Ruiz and the new Nats regime.

The more significant change is in his bat speed, specifically on fastballs compared to offspeed/breaking pitches (to make things easier I’m going to call change ups, curveballs, sliders, etc. offspeed from here on out). If you’ve ever looked at bat speed leaderboards before you may have noticed that players with high contact rates often have slower bat speed. Some of this is likely confirmation bias, players that can’t swing as hard are intentionally choosing contact over power. But some of it is also because these players are adjusting their swing speeds to account for the velocity difference rather than changing when they start to swing.

What I mean by that is, ideally, a hitter would be able to rapidly identify a pitch as a fastball or offspeed pitch, determine whether they think it will be in the strike zone, and then start their swing earlier to square up the fastball or later to square up the offspeed. In reality, even in the Majors, many hitters are starting their swings for a fastball and then slowing down their hands to make contact with an offspeed pitch. This helps keep contact rates up, but the quality of contact is worse.

The last three seasons Ruiz was swinging equally slowly at fastballs and offspeed pitches, around 66.5 MPH. This year he’s still swinging about the same speed at offspeed pitches, but he’s increased his bat speed by 1.5 MPH against fastballs. The change in fastball swing speed is consistent across his platoon split too. Ruiz has clearly made a conscious decision to try to swing harder at fastballs.

Now here’s the part where I would love to tell you that swinging harder at fastballs than breaking balls means a hitter will be better at X or Y. However, looking at hitters who faced at least 500 pitches last season, the only correlation I found is a fairly weak one between positive difference in bat speed for fastballs and isolated slugging percentage, but not enough to make any kind of definitive statement. It’s not as simple as saying swinging harder at fastballs makes you a better hitter.

To understand what the Nats plan is here I need to introduce one other pitch specific change he’s made: swinging a lot less at offspeed pitches. Like with bat speed his swing rate was about the same for fastballs and offspeed pitches the last three seasons, 52 percent. But this year his fastball swing rate is about the same (51 percent), while his offspeed swing rate has dropped to only 48 percent. What’s especially noteworthy is that the change is coming from pitches inside the strike zone, these are pitches Ruiz was likely to make weaker contact with than he would a fastball and end his at-bat.

It should be no surprise that hitters do a lot more damage against fastballs than they do offspeed pitches, last year when batters swung they had an average wOBA of .288 against fastballs and .234 against offspeed pitches. What is happening here is that the Nationals are making an effort to work with the talent they have in Ruiz rather than try to jam him into a one-approach-fits-all mold.

Ruiz is never going to have elite, or even good bat speed. His 68 MPH bat speed against fastballs, despite being 1.5 MPH faster than he has ever swung before, would still be in the bottom 10 in MLB among qualified hitters. However, Ruiz knows how to track a pitch and make contact, by focusing him on maximizing the pitches he swings hard at he can eke out some extra damage.

Overall, this is not going to sustain his current 116 wRC+, but it will likely give him a handful of extra base hits here and there, which paired with his improved defense, could be enough to help the team win. What I’m really heartened by is how the Nats tasked Ruiz with a small, specific, accomplishable goal for improvement, not trying to build a star in a day, but just helping a player be a little better than he was the day before.
